Streamlight TLR-1 HL vs HLX vs TLR-7 HLX: Stop Overthinking Your Weapon Light

Three lights from Streamlight that actually matter, reviewed from the perspective of a responsible concealed carrier who wants to identify a threat, not win a spec sheet argument.

By Brian J. · Observe/Report Original · 2026
Left to right: TLR-1 HLX, TLR-7 HLX, TLR-1 HL

Weapon lights are one of those things people love to overcomplicate. Forums, Reddit, YouTube, everybody arguing lumens, candela, beam patterns like they're running night ops every weekend.

Most of us aren't.

So let's talk about three lights from Streamlight that actually matter: the TLR-1 HL, the one most of us already own; the TLR-1 HLX, the upgraded version that fixes what needed fixing; and the TLR-7 HLX, the one that actually makes sense for concealed carry.

This is not a SWAT review. This is real life, just a review from a responsible concealed carrier who wants to accurately and quickly identify and assess a threat in less than perfect lighting.

The One You Already Have: TLR-1 HL

Let's be honest. The TLR-1 HL has been doing the job for years. It's bright. It's reliable. It's been mounted on everything from nightstand guns to range toys to duty setups. And for most people, it's still enough.

What It Does Well

  • It just works
  • Durable and proven
  • Wide holster support
  • Plenty of light for realistic situations

Where It Falls Behind

  • Candela is lower than newer options
  • Beam can get washed out in busy lighting
  • A little chunky by today's standards

Translation: you're not under-equipped. But you're also not running the best thing available anymore.

The Upgrade That Actually Matters: TLR-1 HLX

The HLX is not a gimmick upgrade. It fixes a real problem. More candela. Tighter beam. More usable light. And that matters way more than people think.

Let's Talk About the Thing Nobody Explains Well

Everyone throws around lumens. Cool, that tells you how much light you have. Candela is what actually lets you use that light. Because real life doesn't happen in a dark, empty room.

You've got lights on inside a house, TV glare, streetlights through windows, reflections bouncing back at you. The HL's lower candela means it throws a wide, relatively diffused beam. That can work against you when your environment is already cluttered with light. You end up with glare and less definition on your target.

The HLX solves this. Higher candela, tighter hotspot, more throw. Your beam cuts through ambient light better and puts more focused illumination where it needs to go.

This isn't theoretical. In a home with normal lighting conditions, the difference is noticeable.

TLR-7 HLX: The Carry Option

Here's the thing about full-size lights on carry guns: they work, but they're not ideal.

The TLR-7 HLX is the first compact light that doesn't feel like a compromise. It brings real output and usable candela in a package that actually makes sense for concealed carry.

If you're carrying concealed, especially AIWB, this is the one that actually makes sense. I've been running it in an Eclipse Sirius holster and the combination works exceptionally well.

Why It Works

  • Compact enough that it doesn't destroy concealability
  • Enough output to matter in real use
  • Sits flush with most carry guns
  • Doesn't make your holster options terrible

The original TLR-7 was fine, but the HLX version brings enough of an upgrade to make it the default choice now.

Which One Should You Actually Get?

TLR-1 HL

Keep it if you already have it. It still works. But if you're buying new, spend the extra and get the HLX.

TLR-1 HLX

Home defense, duty gun, nightstand setup. Anything where concealability doesn't matter and performance does.

TLR-7 HLX

Concealed carry. Period. If you're running a light on an EDC gun, this is the one that makes the most sense.

Real-World Context

Weapon lights aren't about lumens wars or internet points. They're about giving yourself the ability to positively identify what you're looking at before making a decision you can't take back.

For more context on building a complete concealed carry setup, these lights integrate well with the other essentials that round out a serious EDC loadout.

Final Thoughts

Most people don't need to overthink this. They need enough output to identify a threat, enough candela to see clearly under stress, and a setup they'll actually carry consistently. The TLR-1 HL proved itself. The HLX makes it better where it counts. The TLR-7 HLX finally makes compact lights make sense. If your light comes on, it's because something already went wrong. At that point, you don't care about specs. You care about what you can actually see.
